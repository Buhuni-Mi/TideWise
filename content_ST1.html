Beach cleanups: from awareness to action

This page looks at one practical part of SDG 14 using both global and Sri Lankan examples.

On this page

  • Why beach cleanups matter
  • Common waste found on coasts
  • How students can organise a cleanup
  • Safety and responsible disposal
  • Long-term benefits for communities

Why beach cleanups matter

Beach cleanups do more than remove litter. They raise awareness, show how much waste reaches the coast, and help people think about how daily habits affect the sea. They also help people see which waste items are most common in one area.

Common waste found on coasts

Plastic bottles, food wrappers, polythene bags, disposable cups, fishing line, bottle caps, and cigarette waste are often found in beach environments. These items are lightweight, easily transported by wind and water, and difficult for wildlife to avoid.

How students can organise a cleanup

A good cleanup starts with choosing a location, setting a date, getting permission if needed, and preparing gloves and bags. It also helps to give people clear roles such as collecting, sorting, and recording waste. Small groups and short sessions are often easier to manage.

Safety and responsible disposal

Participants should wear gloves, closed shoes, and avoid direct contact with sharp or hazardous items. Waste should be separated where possible, and collected material must be disposed of through proper channels rather than simply moved elsewhere.

Long-term benefits for communities

Regular cleanups keep public spaces cleaner, build shared responsibility, and support tourism and local pride. They can also change behaviour by showing that litter is preventable, not something that should stay on the coast.
