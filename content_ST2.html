Plastic reduction habits for students

This page looks at one practical part of SDG 14 using both global and Sri Lankan examples.

On this page

  • Why single-use plastic is a marine problem
  • Plastic habits students can change today
  • Reusable alternatives
  • Campus and home habits

Why single-use plastic is a marine problem

Single-use plastics are used for a short time, but they can stay in the environment for years. Once they enter drains, waterways, or open spaces, they can easily reach the sea and break into smaller harmful pieces.

Plastic habits students can change today

Students can reduce plastic use by carrying reusable bottles, bringing food containers, refusing unnecessary packaging, and avoiding impulse purchases that come with multiple wrappers. These choices are small individually but powerful when repeated daily.

Reusable alternatives

Reusable bags, bottles, lunch boxes, cutlery sets, and refillable items are practical alternatives. The main goal is not perfection. It is choosing a few common disposable items and replacing them consistently.

Campus and home habits

In campus settings, students can encourage refill stations, cleaner bins, and awareness posters. At home, better shopping habits, more careful separation of waste, and reduced use of disposable dining items can lower the amount of material that becomes litter.

Small changes that scale up

When students show clear plastic reduction habits, other people notice. Friends, classmates, and family members may start doing the same. Repeated action is what turns good habits into normal behaviour.
